The Building

109 Martin Luther King Jr Blvd

Before it was a restaurant, it was a Greyhound Bus Terminal. Built in 1938, it was a place where people arrived and departed, where the rituals of travel made strangers share the same benches and the same waiting. It was also, for much of its history, a building that sorted those people by race.

Mashama and Johno restored the terminal to its original luster, preserving the original gate signage, the oval porthole windows, the porcelain enamel facade, and the chrome details that define the building's Art Deco character. None of that history was hidden. The space continues the building's long tradition of transporting people to a destination, welcoming strangers as friends.

The People

Mashama Bailey and Johno Morisano

Mashama Bailey is a New York City girl, born in the Bronx and raised in Queens, with roots that run deep into Georgia. Johno Morisano is the product of a tight-knit Italian family, born and raised in New York City. In 2015 they opened The Grey together in a 1938 Art Deco Greyhound Bus Terminal, which they painstakingly restored to its original luster, in the heart of Savannah's historic district.

Their story and their partnership became the subject of the 2021 Clarkson Potter book, "Black, White, and The Grey: The Story of an Unexpected Friendship and a Landmark Restaurant." The food, wine and service experience they built here is simultaneously familiar and elevated. Mashama's cuisine is Port City Southern, drawing on local seafood, regional produce, and the deep, layered, and soulful flavors of this coast. The Grey earned Eater's Restaurant of the Year in 2017 and 2018. Mashama was named Best Chef Southeast by the James Beard Foundation in 2019.
